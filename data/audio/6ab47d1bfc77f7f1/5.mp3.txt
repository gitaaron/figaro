So far in this course, you have built up a really solid foundation. You understand conditional probability, the chain rule, how neural networks learn representations, and what encoders and decoders actually do. You have also seen how autoregressive models predict one token at a time, always asking the same question: given everything I have seen so far, what comes next? Now we are going to zoom in on something beautiful that sits at the heart of all of that — autoregressive encoding, and what it really means to capture context as you read through a sequence.

Let's start with a simple image. Imagine you are reading a sentence one word at a time, and as you read each word, you are mentally updating your understanding of what the sentence is about. When you read the word 'the', you don't know much yet. When you then read 'cat', you have a little more to work with. When you read 'sat', now you have a subject and a verb. And when you finally read 'on the mat', the whole picture is clear. At every single step, your understanding — your mental model of the sentence — has been updated by the new word you just read, combined with everything you already knew. That is exactly what an autoregressive encoder does.

In earlier lessons, we talked about hidden states — those internal representations that a neural network builds up as it processes information. Think of the hidden state as the encoder's running summary of the sequence. At the very beginning, before any tokens have been processed, the hidden state is essentially a blank slate, usually initialized to zeros or some learned starting value. Then the encoder reads the first token, and it updates the hidden state to reflect what it has seen. It reads the second token, and it updates the hidden state again, this time combining the new token with the previous hidden state. It keeps doing this, one token at a time, all the way to the end of the sequence.

The key insight here is that the hidden state at any given step is not just a representation of the current token in isolation. It is a representation of the current token in the context of everything that came before it. So when the encoder processes the word 'bank' in a sentence, the hidden state it produces doesn't just say 'this is the word bank.' It says something much richer, like 'this is the word bank, and based on the words that came before it, we are probably talking about a financial institution, not a riverbank.' The context shapes the meaning, and the hidden state captures that shaped meaning.

This is what makes autoregressive encoding so powerful for understanding language and sequences. Meaning is almost never local. The meaning of a word depends on what surrounds it, and especially on what came before it. By updating the hidden state at every single step, the autoregressive encoder is essentially threading context through the entire sequence, carrying forward everything that matters.

Now you might wonder — does the hidden state remember everything perfectly? And the honest answer is, not exactly. One of the classic challenges with this approach is that information from early in a sequence can get diluted or compressed as more and more tokens are processed. If a sentence is very long, the hidden state at the end might not hold onto fine details from the very beginning quite as sharply. Researchers have developed clever solutions to this problem, like attention mechanisms and more sophisticated architectures, but the core idea of left-to-right context accumulation remains foundational.

It is also worth appreciating the directional nature of this process. Autoregressive encoding moves strictly from left to right, or from the beginning of a sequence to the end. Each hidden state only reflects what came before that position — never what comes after. This is actually a deliberate and important property. It means the encoder can be used to make predictions at each step without peeking at the future, which is exactly what you need when you are generating text or making real-time decisions.

So to bring it all together: an autoregressive encoder reads a sequence one token at a time, and at each step it produces a hidden state that is a rich, context-aware representation of everything up to that point. Early tokens set the stage, and every subsequent token refines and updates the picture. By the time the encoder reaches the final token, the hidden state it produces carries a compressed but meaningful summary of the entire sequence. That final hidden state, or sometimes the collection of all the hidden states along the way, becomes the foundation for whatever task you are trying to accomplish — whether that is translation, classification, generation, or something else entirely.
